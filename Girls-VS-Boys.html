Girls vs Boys - X Std Exam performance, Karnataka, India

Kaveri Nadhamuni - August 2014

Did girls fare better than boys in the Karnataka board exam results in the year 2011? In this study I am trying to compare the academic performance of girls and boys in Karnataka who took the 10th standard state board(SSLC) exams. I started by analyzing the dataset which consisted of 855,446 students who took the SSLC state board exams in 2012.

Even though I have found correlations between specific factors(such as gender, medium of instruction etc) they might not be the underlying cause of the corresponding academic performance. These results I have found may have been caused by other hidden parameters that are not present/studied in the dataset. I have come up with an hypothesis as to the results of this paper and found other articles and studies that cooraborate it.

Summary of the Dataset

From this summary, we can see that the number of girls and boys is roughly the same and are approxiamately split 50-50. Other significant facts about the dataset are that math, science and social science are the most common subjects among students. Most students seem to take Kannada as the medium of instruction illustrated by 82% of them taking Kannada as their 1st language. Most of them also took English as their 2nd language and Hindi as their 3rd language.

The state of Karnataka had a population of 61 million in 2011, accounting for about 13million families (family size is 4.6). So about 1 in 17 Karnataka families had a Xth grader who took these board exams.

The above data shows that the girls have scored higher than the boys in every subject. It would be interesting to find why the gilrs are doing consistently better than the boys. Also this trend is not specific to India and seems happens all over the world. A recent study[1] was conducted by psychologists at Glasgow and Missouri universities of 1.5 million students in 74 regions around the world. Amazingly it found that the girls outperformed the boys in maths, reading, science and literary subjects in 70% of the 74 regions, irrespective of social, political and economic status. The possible reasons could be:

1) Girls spend more time on homework than boys. According to a study by the OECD[2], boys spend 1 hour less per week on homework than girls on average. In India, this could be due to the fact that girls usually engage in domestic work at home whereas boys usually work outside their homes by helping their parents with ther work, buying goods or helping out in the field. Working at home may be more conducive to studying than working outside the home, which may be why girls have more of an opportunity to do schoolwork and hence imrpove their academic performance.

2) Girls are observed to read more than boys. According to PISA (Programme for International Student Assessment of the OECD) 2009, girls read more than boys do. This may explain the large disparity between the girls' and boys' scores in language subjects in the bar chart above. 

3) Another factor that could contribute to the girls' superior academic performance could be teacher bias. Teachers are biased towards girls and tend to mark up their work. In anonymous tests [3] where the gender of the student is unknown to the teacher, it is found that the gender gap reduces by a third, suggesting that this leniency in marking is an implicit reward for girls who are perceived to be more school-friendly.

The girls have done particularly well compared to boys in the language subjects, the gap between the mean scores being: English - 15.46, Kannada - 10.97, Urdu - 15.11 and Hindi - 16.11.

Many studies have shown that girls are more inclined towards language and linguistics than boys. A study published in the Scientific American [4] fortified this hypothesis: 'Girls completing a linguistic abilities task showed greater activity in brain areas implicated specifically in language encoding, which decipher information abstractly. Boys, on the other hand, showed a lot of activity in regions tied to visual and auditory functions'(http://www.scientificamerican.com/article/are-women-really-better-with-language/). The study supports the inferences of the dataset that the girls' superior performance in the language subjects ( English, Kannada, Hindi, Urdu) and their average performance in the analytical subjects (Math, Social Science, Science, Economics).

Awarding Grace Marks

As we can see, there are two spikes at 30 and 35 on the histograms for most subjects that disrupt the otherwise smooth normal distribution curve. In the Continuous and Comprehensive Evaluation (CCE) system for SSLC, the pass mark is 30 out of 100 in each paper in the public exam, but they need to maintain an aggregate of 35 per cent in six subjects to pass overall. It appears that the teachers and examiners seem to have awarded grace marks to many students to ensure they pass the examination. There are very few students who got marks between 26 and 29- they seem to have been 'pushed' to the 30th mark instead. There are also very few students with 31 to 34 marks since the teachers have 'pushed' them to the overall pass milestone which is 35 marks.

Different studies on ICSE, CBSE and SSLC (Indian central and state education boards) have proven the same behaviour of awarding grace marks. Debarghya Das, a student from Cornell, revealed trend of awarding grace marks [5] in the ICSE board as well . This trend does not seem to be isolated to India alone. A study in Poland of students' marks [6] in a standardized test, 'Matura', shows a similar skewed distribution, with spikes at the pass mark .

Conclusion:

From the above analysis, one may conclude that the girls did better than the boys in every subject, especially the languages. This is attributed to girls' higher linguistic capabilites. Looking at the histograms,we can see that there is a skewed normal distribution and that there are spikes in the pass marks. This implies that the examiners have been liberally pushing students marks up to reach the pass mark. This pattern of pushing students marks up has been found in many other studies of other exams as well.
